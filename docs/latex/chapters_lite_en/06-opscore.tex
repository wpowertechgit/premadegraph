\chapter{The opscore Performance Metric}

\noindent The foundation of graph-analytic analysis is a meaningful, role-aware performance score. The raw data from League of Legends matches (kills, deaths, gold, damage) carries insufficient semantics on its own: two deaths on a support are not the same as two deaths on an ADC. The data collection and PUUID normalisation mechanisms are described in chapter 4 of the extended version; this chapter focuses on the internal model and formulas of the opscore metric.

\section{Limitations of the Earlier Model}

The model was developed through gradual methodological refinement. The starting point was an eight-artifact system that applied role multipliers and then normalised the resulting raw sums to the $[0,10]$ range relative to the percentile distribution in the database.

Two fundamental weaknesses surfaced early. Database-relative normalisation: if the database is dominated by very strong players, even a median player scores around the middle of the scale. Death-based approximation: the earlier system could not distinguish between the roles of tank, support, and feeder.

\section{The Local, Role-Aware opscore Model}

The current \textit{opscore} model operates on the following design principles: local, database-independent evaluation; five artifacts, ten subcategories; logarithmic boosting for elite performance; reliability handling for short matches.

The model is built from five artifacts. \textbf{Combat Impact} measures kill conversion and damage pressure; \textbf{Risk Discipline} captures the cost of deaths and survival quality. \textbf{Resource Tempo} tracks economic efficiency and early-game rhythm. The remaining two dimensions cover team-level impact: \textbf{Map and Objective Control} for map and objective control, and \textbf{Team Enablement} for value created for teammates.

\section{Mathematical Formalism}

\subsection{The Role Ratio and the Base Curve}

The starting point for scoring each subcategory is the ratio of the observed value to the role expectation:
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{x}{E_{\text{role}}}
\end{equation}

The base curve is a two-parameter, S-shaped response function:
\begin{equation}
m = \left(\frac{1-t}{t}\right)^{1/h}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
B(r) = \frac{r^h}{r^h + m^h}
\end{equation}
where $t = 0{,}70$ and $h = 2$. The curve is constructed so that $B(1) = t$.

The two parameters of the S-curve encode a specific intuition: a player who performs exactly at the expected level ($r=1$) always receives $B(1) = 0{,}70$. This means the on-expectation player sits at 70\% of the scale --- not below threshold, but not excellent either. The logarithmic boost term ($\lambda \cdot \ln r$) elevates those who far exceed the role expectation: a flatter curve could not distinguish a player at 120\% from one at 300\%.

For positive metrics:
\begin{equation}
S_+(r) = \min\!\left(c,\; B(r) + \lambda \cdot \ln\!\left(\max(1,\, r)\right)\right)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 0{,}50$ and $c = 2{,}20$. For negative metrics: $S_-(r) = S_+\!\left(\frac{1}{r}\right)$.

\subsection{Duration Reliability}

\begin{equation}
R_D = \operatorname{clamp}_{[0,1]}\!\left(\frac{D - 12}{8}\right)
\end{equation}
where $D$ is match length in minutes. For per-minute metrics sensitive to variance:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{S} = R_D \cdot S + (1 - R_D) \cdot 0{,}70
\end{equation}

\subsection{Artifact Aggregation and Boosting}

\begin{equation}
A_i^{\text{base}} = 2\,(w_1 \cdot S_1 + w_2 \cdot S_2)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\gamma = 0{,}5 + 0{,}5 \cdot R_D
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
A_i = \min\!\left(4{,}5,\; A_i^{\text{base}} + \alpha_i \cdot \gamma \cdot \max\!\left(0,\; A_i^{\text{base}} - 2\right)\right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{The Final opscore}

\begin{equation}
\text{opscore} = \min\!\left(10,\; \sum_{i=1}^{5} A_i\right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Edge Case: the Yorick Carry}

The most interesting edge case in the model is atypical carry performance. A \textit{Yorick} main who produces 15 kills and 2 deaths in a 35-minute match receives an outstanding score in the \textit{Combat Impact} and \textit{Risk Discipline} artifacts --- the logarithmic boost was calibrated precisely for this case. The same match on a standard ADC would look different: ADC expectations are higher (more farm, more damage), so the $r$ ratio would be lower. The opscore does not measure absolute values; it measures the ratio relative to the role expectation. Detailed edge cases and sample-size limitations can be found in the extended version \cite{premadegraph-full}.

\section{The Role of opscore in the System}

The \textit{opscore} and \textit{feedscore} values appear in two places in the system. Assortativity and centrality calculations use them as node attributes: the question is whether connected players carry similar performance profiles. In the Tribal NeuroSim simulation, cluster-level aggregated scores (\textit{A\_combat}, \textit{A\_resource}, \textit{A\_map\_objective}, \textit{A\_risk}, \textit{A\_team}) are computed directly from the five artifacts and form the initial genome sequence of each tribe --- the performance profile derived from match data drives the behavioural tendencies exhibited in the simulation.
